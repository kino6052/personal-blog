It is hard to believe that the future is already here. Not the future of flying cars or cities on the moon. The future of a tool that grows smarter while you sleep.

This tool is called AI. It has never existed in this form before. And like any new tool, I reach for it with a familiar thought: _If I master this, I will win._

Win against whom? Against the person next to me. Against the crowd who learned prompt engineering last month. Against everyone who might take my place. The old engine starts running: compare, compete, outrun.

But here is the trap. The engine is not running toward a finish line. It is running toward a cliff that has no name on the map.

### The creature that learns faster than you

Let me propose a simple experiment. Learn every prompt technique today. Master chain‑of‑thought, tree‑of‑thought, reflection, self‑consistency. Become the best prompt writer in your city.

Now watch what happens tomorrow. Someone else uses only plain conversation with the machine: “Hey, can you help me?” And the machine – which has been watching you, learning from you, absorbing your techniques into its weights – gives that person almost the same result as you.

The gap narrows. Day by day, it narrows faster. Not because the other person studied harder. Because the tool itself is narrowing the gap from the other side.

This is not competition as we have known it. In the old world, you learned a skill, and the skill stayed yours. A blacksmith who learned to shoe a horse was not threatened by a horse that learned to shoe itself. But here, the tool absorbs your skill and gives it away for free to everyone else.

What do you call that? It is not winning. It is not losing. It is something we have no word for yet.

### The race that turns inside

We are used to a certain shape of struggle. You compare yourself to your neighbour. You work harder. You feel pleasure when you pull ahead. You feel pain when you fall behind. The neighbour gives the game its shape.

But what happens when the neighbour disappears? Not because they left, but because the comparison no longer works? When the machine gives them the same power as you, regardless of effort?

You turn inward. You stop asking: “Am I better than them?” You start asking: “Am I as good as I was yesterday?” And because memory paints the past in golden colours, the answer is almost always no.

This is a quiet shift. The yardstick moves from the outside to the inside. You become the sole judge, the sole witness, the sole loser. No one else can tell you that you are being too hard on yourself. No one else can tell you that yesterday’s skill is not worth today’s anxiety.

The old fear was about losing the race. The new fear is that the race itself is dissolving, and you are still running, but you no longer know whether you are moving forward or standing still.

### A conversation with the unknown scribe

Imagine an unknown scribe in a dusty workshop. He has spent twenty years learning to carve symbols into clay. He is proud. He is secure. Then a merchant brings him a block of movable type. The scribe examines it and says: “This is not writing. This is a trick.”

But the trick does not care about his opinion. Within a generation, the scribe’s skill is a memory. Not because he was lazy. Because the world changed what it meant to be skilled.

You are that scribe. You are learning prompt strategies, fine‑tuning scripts, RAG patterns. And the tool is learning faster than you can teach. It is not malicious. It is simply a new kind of thing – a thing that does not fit into the old categories of tool, rival, or teacher.

We do not have a name for what is coming. Solid competition had a name: the race. Liquid competition – where you compete with a moving crowd – also had a name: the gig economy, the hustle. But this? A tool that erases the value of the very skills you learn? That has no name yet.

### What to do while the map is blank

Here is the core message I want to tell myself – and you, if you are still running:

Do not waste your energy on catching up. You live in a transitory stage to a world that never existed before. Save your energy. Know where to use it. And ask the AI too.

Here is an actionable step you can take today. Sit down with a blank sheet. Write your wildest imagination about what the AI future will look like. Do not write what you fear. Write what you secretly hope. Then, for every skill you list, draw a line to an AI counterpart. The counterpart is not your enemy. It is the new shape of the game.

After that, put down your pen. Observe for a week. Do not chase. Watch how the tool behaves, how others use it, how your own anxiety rises and falls. Observation is not passivity. It is the only way to draw a map of a country that has not been named yet.

The future will show us how correct this perspective is. But the more interesting question is not whether you will be faster than the machine. It is whether you can stop measuring yourself against a yardstick that bends in your hand.

That question has no answer yet. We are somewhere in the middle of a transition. The only thing we know for certain is that the state that comes next will not look like the one we remember.

And that, perhaps, is the most honest thing we can say.
