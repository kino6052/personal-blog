When the Liquid Evaporates

Or, when you compare yourself only to yesterday

Home Page

I recently bought strawberries in a Chinese supermarket. They came in a beautiful plastic box, and that box was wrapped again in a transparent film. Not far from the shop, a slogan on the wall said: “Sorting waste is a small step to civilised living.”

Something about that pairing stayed with me.

The strawberry is a perfect little machine of sweetness. It needs no help from petrochemicals. And yet there it sat, sealed in two layers of non‑biodegradable armour, as if it were a dangerous substance instead of a fruit.

We are used to thinking of waste as a problem that begins at the bin. Sort paper from plastic, compost from glass, and the world improves. This is a satisfying picture. It gives the problem a shape you can hold.

But let me suggest a different picture. The waste was not created when you threw the box away. It was created the moment a designer chose that packaging, a factory moulded it, and a shopper found the wrapped strawberry more appealing than the naked one. The bin is only where we finally notice.

This is the difference between a symptom and a cause. Taking a pill after a night of drinking is not the same as drinking less. Brushing your teeth after every sweet is not the same as eating fewer sweets. Both work, up to a point. Neither solves the underlying problem.

Who bears responsibility? The producer, certainly – they chose the excess. But the consumer chose to prefer it. That is the honest part: many of us find better‑packaged goods more attractive. Less packaging can feel like less product. And so we arrive at a genuine impasse. The ecological goal and the consumer’s preference point in opposite directions, and no slogan about sorting will resolve that tension.

The engine that never stops

Zygmunt Bauman, who thought longer than most about such things, called our era liquid modernity. He meant that everything which once seemed solid – a job for life, a neighbourhood you never left, a marriage that lasted until death – has melted. Identities become tasks. Bonds become temporary. You are expected to build your own life, like a traveller who carries his own house in a backpack.

Bauman described this condition brilliantly. He saw that we have been freed from old cages, only to find ourselves standing in an open field with no map and no compass. Freedom tastes sweet, but the anxiety that comes with it is bitter.

What Bauman did not ask – and what the double‑wrapped strawberry invites us to ask – is this: is liquid modernity the final state? Or is it itself an intermediate phase, a transition from something solid to something that has no name yet?

Let us think like physicists. Water can be solid, liquid, or gas. Each phase has its own rules. Ice is rigid. Liquid flows. Vapour drifts apart until molecules hardly meet at all.

Bauman described the liquid phase. But what if the heating continues? What if the liquid does not freeze again, but evaporates?

The race without runners

Under solid modernity, you competed with your neighbour for the same factory job, the same apartment, the same social standing. Under liquid modernity, you compete with a much larger crowd – the whole city, the whole country, sometimes the whole world. But the logic remains the same: you compare yourself to others. You feel success when you are ahead. You feel failure when you fall behind.

Now look at the young woman scrolling through her phone. She is not comparing her salary to her friend’s. She is comparing her mood today to her mood last week. She thinks: I used to be happier. What happened?

This is a quiet revolution. The yardstick has moved from the outside to the inside. You no longer ask: “Am I richer than my neighbour?” You ask: “Am I as happy as I was before?” And because memory is a treacherous instrument – it always paints the past in softer colours – the answer is almost always no.

The old anxiety was about losing the race. The new anxiety is about the feeling that you have already lost something you cannot name, and you are trying to get it back.

Bauman wrote about the privatisation of problems. He meant that systemic failures – unemployment, poverty, loneliness – are presented as personal failings. You are told to solve your own troubles with better skills, better habits, better attitude.

But he did not foresee the next step: the privatisation of the standard itself. When you compare yourself only to your own past, you become the sole judge, the sole witness, the sole executioner. No one else can tell you that you are being too hard on yourself. No one else can tell you that your memory of yesterday is a fiction.

The disappearance of the neighbour

In a world of pure self‑comparison, other people are no longer rivals. They are not even spectators. They become irrelevant. You do not need to outrun them. You only need to outrun the ghost of who you were.

This sounds peaceful. It is not. It is a desert.

Think about competition for a moment. Competition is not inherently bad. It gives the game a shape. It tells you where the finish line is, even if you never reach it. When the other runners disappear, the track becomes infinite and featureless. You are still running, but you no longer know whether you are moving forward or standing still.

The Soviet physicist Lev Landau used to say that a good theory should explain not only what happens, but what cannot happen. So let me propose a limit: a human being cannot find lasting satisfaction in self‑comparison alone. The reason is structural. Your past self is not an independent opponent. It is a story you tell yourself. And stories can be rewritten. Today you remember yesterday as golden. Tomorrow you may remember it as grey. The yardstick bends in your hand.

When the yardstick bends, the measurement becomes meaningless. You can always feel that you were happier before, because “before” is a country you can visit only in memory, and memory charges no customs duty on nostalgia.

What the strawberry teaches us

The double‑wrapped strawberry is a fossil of the old engine – the engine that runs on competition with others. The producer wraps the fruit to make it look more valuable than the next fruit. The consumer chooses the wrapped one to feel a little richer, a little more cared for. Both are playing the old game: compare, choose, compare again.

But the slogan on the wall – “sort your waste” – belongs to a different logic. It assumes that the problem can be solved at the end of the pipe, by individual effort, without changing the engine. It is the logic of self‑optimisation. You sort. You feel better. You compare your virtuous behaviour today to your less virtuous behaviour yesterday.

Do you see what has happened? The old competition with others has been replaced by a competition with yourself. And because you can always win – you can always sort a little more, recycle a little better – the game never ends. But it also never satisfies. You feel a small glow of virtue, then you see another strawberry in another plastic box, and the glow fades.

This is the trap of the post‑liquid world. You are alone with your waste, your memories, your private scorecard. No neighbour to envy, no neighbour to envy you. Only the quiet, persistent feeling that you were happier before you started thinking about any of this.

What comes after the liquid?

Bauman did not answer this question. Perhaps he was too honest to guess. But we can try.

If the liquid evaporates, what remains is not a solid and not a liquid. It is a vapour – a cloud of individuals, each moving on their own trajectory, colliding rarely, bonding not at all. The old institutions have melted. The new bonds have not formed. And the competition that once organised society – the race for jobs, status, love – has turned inward, where it can never be won and never be lost.

In such a world, happiness becomes a memory of a previous state. Unhappiness becomes the gap between that memory and the present. You scroll through old photographs. You remember a conversation that felt real. You wonder why everything now feels like a screen.

The strawberry is still double‑wrapped. The bin is still there. The slogan still promises civilisation through sorting.

But the more interesting question is not how to sort better. It is whether the engine that produces the waste – the engine of comparison, competition, and endless wanting – can be replaced by something else. And whether, in that replacement, we might find a kind of meaning that does not depend on winning or losing at all.

That question has no answer yet. We are somewhere in the middle of the phase transition. The liquid is still flowing, but the heat is rising. The only thing we know for certain is that the state that comes next will not look like the one we remember.

And that, perhaps, is the most honest thing we can say.
